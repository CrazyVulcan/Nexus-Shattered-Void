NEXUS: Shattered Void
Rules Primer

VERSION 0.9 — RULES PRIMER (WORKING DRAFT)

This rules primer covers the confirmed core mechanics of Nexus: Shattered Void — a tactical starship combat game centered on subsystem degradation, power allocation, and progressive capability loss. Content labeled [Placeholder] is a working assumption subject to change.

2+ Players d6 Only SSD-Centered v0.9 Draft

1. Introduction

Nexus: Shattered Void is a tactical starship combat wargame centered on subsystem warfare, power allocation, and progressive capability loss. Ships are not abstract hit-point pools — they are machines under pressure. Damage changes what you can do, not just how close you are to dead.

Design Intent

  • Reduce friction. The SSD is the primary play surface. No separate tracking sheets, no looking things up mid-battle.
  • Preserve depth. Every power decision matters. Every subsystem hit forces a trade-off.
  • Make damage meaningful. Losing an Engineering threshold costs you power. Losing a Weapons threshold degrades your firepower. Ships feel like machines breaking down under fire.
  • Tactical lifespan. Subsystem tracks communicate how long a ship can remain functionally dangerous, not just physically alive.

The SSD

Each ship is represented by a Ship System Display (SSD). The SSD is not a reference card. It is your primary gameplay interface for that ship. It tracks:

  • All subsystem damage tracks (Engineering, FTL, Weapons)
  • Current power level
  • d6 hit allocation table (which subsystem gets hit on each d6 result)
  • Weapon stats and degradation state

Use the SSD Builder to design and print your ships.

Note: Ship factions, detailed setting lore, and advanced campaign rules are working assumptions in this draft and are subject to change. Only the confirmed mechanics in this primer are canonical.

2. Components

Nexus: Shattered Void is system-agnostic. You supply your own miniatures or ship tokens; the rules work with any suitable models.

Required Components

  • Ship models or tokens (one per ship in your fleet)
  • 6-sided dice (d6) only — at least 10 per player recommended; all dice rolls in the game use d6
  • Measuring tape or ruler (in inches)
  • Ship System Displays (SSD) — printable from the Ship Builder on this website
  • Power allocation markers (poker chips, tokens, or counters) — 6–8 per ship recommended
  • Damage markers (for tracking filled track boxes)
  • Shield tokens (one set per facing per ship)
  • Ammo tokens (for tracking secondary weapon ammo)

Optional Components

  • Terrain: asteroids, space stations, derelict hulks, debris fields, nebulae
  • Scenario objective markers
  • Fleet roster sheets

Dice Notation

This rulebook uses standard dice notation: XdY means roll X dice with Y faces. Because Nexus uses only six-sided dice, all rolls in this game are expressed as Xd6.

Ship System Displays (SSDs): Each ship's track boxes, threshold markers, shields, weapons, and subsystems are recorded on its SSD card. Use the free Ship Builder tool to generate, customise, and print SSDs for every hull in your fleet.

3. Game Setup

Preparing the Battlefield

Nexus: Shattered Void is played on a flat surface. The standard game area is 48" × 48" (4' × 4'). For larger fleets or narrative scenarios, 60" × 48" is recommended.

  1. Agree on a point limit for the game (standard: 300 points; narrative: 500+; skirmish: 150).
  2. Place terrain. Each player alternates placing one terrain piece until both agree the board is ready, or follow the scenario terrain rules.
  3. Determine deployment zones. In standard play, each player deploys from within 8" of their table edge.
  4. Players roll off (highest roll goes first). The winner chooses their deployment zone.
  5. Players alternate deploying ships one at a time, starting with the player who won the roll-off.
  6. The player who deployed last takes the first turn.

5. Turn Structure

Every round of Nexus: Shattered Void follows the same three-phase sequence. Understanding the distinction between phases is essential—decisions made in Phase 1 cannot be changed once Phase 2 begins.

Phase 1 — Power Allocation (Simultaneous)

Both players simultaneously and secretly allocate power markers to each of their ships.

  • Each ship generates power equal to its Engineering ÷ 3 (rounded down). This is listed on the ship's SSD as its Base Power.
  • Typical values: Corvettes generate 2–3 power; Cruisers generate 4–5 power.
  • Power may be allocated to any of the following uses:
    • Attack Resource — enables the ship to fire weapons this activation.
    • Targeting Boost — improves hit chance (vs. Agility gate).
    • Weapon Power Boost — improves damage penetration (vs. Toughness gate).
    • Shield Boost — temporarily increases shield values on one or more facings.
    • Repair — restores boxes on a chosen damage track.
    • System Usage — activates a ship subsystem (e.g., FTL, Probes).
  • Each stat boost category may only be used once per round per ship.
  • Attack resource is separate from stat boosts — spending power on a Targeting Boost does not consume the attack resource, and vice versa.
  • All allocations are committed before Phase 2 begins. Targets are not chosen during allocation.
Key Principle: You must decide how to spend power before you know what you will shoot. This forces predictive decision-making—the hallmark of skilled play.

Phase 2 — Ship Activation (Alternating)

Starting with the player who has Priority, players alternate activating one ship at a time until all ships have been activated. When you activate a ship:

  1. Spend attack resource based on opportunity (targets must be in arc and range).
  2. Fire weapons — choose targets now; range and arc must be valid at the moment of firing.
  3. Move the ship — movement occurs after firing, not before.
⚠️ Important: A ship moves after it fires. You must position correctly in a previous round to have the correct arcs and range this round. Nexus rewards planning one turn ahead.

Priority at the start of each round is held by the player whose fleet has fewer remaining ships. On a tie, Priority passes to the player who did not hold it last round.

Phase 3 — Reset

  • Remove all temporary power allocations and boost tokens.
  • Persistent states are maintained: ammo spent, track damage filled, and any permanent degradation remain in place.
  • Begin the next round with Phase 1.

6. Power Economy

Power is the central resource of the game. Every meaningful action a ship takes—firing weapons, boosting defences, repairing damage—costs power. Managing your power budget across all ships is the primary strategic challenge of the Power Allocation phase.

Generating Power

A ship's available power each round equals its Engineering track value ÷ 3 (rounded down). This value is printed on the SSD as Base Power. As the Engineering track degrades (see Section 9), maximum power decreases.

Ship TypeTypical PowerTypical Engineering
Corvette / Interceptor2–36–9
Destroyer / Frigate39–11
Cruiser4–512–15
Capital Ship / Dreadnought515+

Spending Power

During Power Allocation, each power token must be assigned to one of these uses. Unspent power is wasted—it does not carry over to the next round.

UseEffectLimit
Attack ResourceEnables firing of weapons this activation (each weapon lists its power cost)One token per weapon firing
Targeting BoostAdd +1 to all dice rolls against the Agility gate this activationOnce per ship per round
Weapon Power BoostAdd +1 to all dice rolls against the Toughness gate this activationOnce per ship per round
Shield BoostTemporarily increase shields on one facing by 1 (removed in Reset phase; does not restore permanently depleted shields)Once per ship per round
RepairRestore 1 filled box on any one of the four damage tracks (Structure, Engineering, Weapons, or Systems)Once per ship per round
System UsageActivates a subsystem (FTL, Probes, Aux Craft, etc.) as per its rulesVaries by subsystem

Key Rule: Attack Resource vs. Stat Boosts

Spending power on a Targeting or Weapon Power boost does not consume the attack resource—these are separate expenditures. A ship that allocates 1 power to Targeting Boost and 2 power to fire its primary weapon has used 3 power total.

Example (Cruiser, 4 power):
1 power → Attack Resource (primary weapon, cost 1)
2 power → Attack Resource (torpedo, cost 2)
1 power → Targeting Boost
Total: 4 power spent. No power remains for repairs or shields this round—a deliberate trade-off.

7. Movement & Arcs

Movement in Nexus is measured in inches from the front of the ship's base to the front of where it ends up. Crucially, movement occurs after firing—you fire with your current position, then reposition for the next round.

Movement Rules

  • Ships move up to their Speed value in inches.
  • Ships may turn freely during movement; there is no facing restriction unless a special rule applies.
  • Ships may not move through other ships (friendly or enemy).
  • Ships may not end their movement inside terrain.

Fire Arcs

Each ship has four fire arcs—Fore, Aft, Port, and Starboard—defined by the weapon's listed arc. A weapon may only fire at targets within its arc at the moment of firing. Shields are also tracked per facing, so the arc from which an attack comes determines which shield pool is tested first.

Range

Weapons list a minimum range and a maximum range. If the target is closer than the minimum range, the weapon cannot fire (e.g., torpedoes cannot be used at point-blank distance). Measuring range and confirming the correct arc before committing power is essential—mistakes cannot be corrected after allocation.

⚠️ Remember: Because you fire before you move, you must spend the current round positioning so that next round your chosen weapons have valid range and arc on likely targets. Nexus rewards anticipation.

8. Combat

Combat in Nexus: Shattered Void uses a two-gate dice system. Every attack rolls a pool of d6; each die must clear two sequential thresholds before it deals damage. There is no single "to-hit" roll—instead, the dice are filtered through the target's Agility and then its Toughness.

Step 1 — Roll the Dice Pool

The attacker rolls a number of d6 equal to the firing weapon's Dice value (printed on the weapon entry of the SSD).

Step 2 — Gate 1: Agility Check

Each die is compared against the target ship's Agility stat. A die meets or exceeds the Agility value — it survives to Gate 2. Any die that falls below the Agility value is discarded (a miss).

If the attacker spent a power token on a Targeting Boost this round, add +1 to every die result before comparing to Agility.

Step 3 — Gate 2: Toughness Check

Surviving dice are now compared against the target ship's Toughness stat. A die meets or exceeds the Toughness value — it becomes a damage event. Dice that fall below Toughness are discarded (absorbed by armour).

If the attacker spent a power token on a Weapon Power Boost this round, add +1 to every surviving die result before comparing to Toughness.

Damage Events

Each die that passes both gates is one damage event. Damage events are resolved in sequence; see Section 9 for how shields and tracks are affected.

Example Attack — Laser Battery (3 dice) vs. Phantom-class Interceptor (Agility 5, Toughness 2):
Roll: 2, 4, 6
Gate 1 (Agility 5): 2 fails, 4 fails, 6 passes → 1 die survives
Gate 2 (Toughness 2): 6 passes → 1 damage event
Same attack with Targeting Boost (+1 to Gate 1):
Roll: 2, 4, 6 → add +1 → 3, 5, 7
Gate 1 (Agility 5): 3 fails, 5 passes, 7 passes → 2 dice survive
Gate 2 (Toughness 2): both pass → 2 damage events

Firing Multiple Weapons

A ship may fire multiple weapons during its activation, provided it spent the required attack resource power token for each weapon. Each weapon is resolved as a separate dice pool; Targeting Boost and Weapon Power Boost apply to all pools fired this activation (since they were purchased once during allocation).

Primary Weapons & Repeatable Fire

A ship's Primary Weapon has no ammo limit and is marked repeatable. If the ship has spare attack resource power tokens, it may fire its primary weapon multiple times in one activation.

9. Damage & Subsystem Tracks

Ships in Nexus do not have a single hit-point pool. Each ship has a set of independent subsystem tracks. When a ship is hit and damage penetrates its defenses, a d6 is rolled and the result is cross-referenced against that ship's d6 Hit Allocation table on its SSD. The matching subsystem track takes one box of damage.

Key Principle: The hit allocation table is defined per ship, printed on its SSD. There is no universal table. Each ship design distributes d6 values across its tracks differently — making faction and hull design meaningful.

Confirmed Subsystem Tracks

Every ship has at minimum:

  • Engineering Track — Power generation capacity. Crossing a threshold permanently reduces the ship's maximum available power for all future rounds. Example pattern: 3→2→1→0.
  • FTL Track — FTL drive integrity. As this track degrades the ship loses its ability to jump. When fully degraded the FTL drive is disabled.
  • Primary Weapon Track — The ship's main weapon system. The track degrades in steps — reducing firepower before the weapon is lost entirely.

Ships may additionally carry:

  • Secondary Weapon Tracks — One track per secondary weapon system. Each secondary degrades independently.
  • Generic Subsystem Tracks — Used for sensors, command, shields, or any other custom subsystem defined in the ship's design.

Engineering Degradation & Power

Engineering is the most critical track. Each threshold crossed reduces the ship's maximum power by 1, permanently for the rest of the battle.

The engineering track's power levels are printed on the SSD beside the track. Cross-hatch (threshold) markers show where the power drops occur visually.

Weapon Track Degradation

Weapons degrade in steps rather than flipping from active to destroyed. As a weapon track fills:

  • Each threshold crossed degrades the weapon by one step (e.g., losing one die from its attack pool, or reducing range, or increasing power cost — specific effects are defined per weapon on the SSD).
  • The weapon is fully disabled only when all its track boxes are filled.

This means a damaged weapon is still dangerous — it just costs more or hits less reliably.

FTL Degradation

The FTL track works the same as any subsystem track. When all boxes are filled the ship cannot jump. When thresholds are crossed the ship's jump capability may be restricted (e.g., shorter jump range, longer spin-up time — [specific effects are a working assumption, subject to design]).

The d6 Hit Allocation Table

Each ship's SSD includes a small table showing which d6 roll maps to which subsystem track. For example:

d6Track Hit
1–2Engineering
3FTL Drive
4–5Primary Weapon
6Secondary Weapon

This table is defined per ship when the ship is designed in the SSD Builder. More engineering-focused hit numbers create a ship that is especially vulnerable to power loss. Heavier weighting on weapons creates a ship that loses firepower quickly under pressure. The designer chooses the distribution.

Threshold Markers (Cross-Hatch)

On each track, threshold positions are shown as cross-hatched boxes instead of empty boxes. When a threshold box is filled, the degradation effect for that track triggers immediately. This is the primary visual signal on the SSD — degrade enough and the cross-hatch pattern shows the severity at a glance.

Tactical Lifespan

Because damage goes to specific subsystems rather than an abstract pool, a ship can remain physically intact but be tactically crippled — out of power, weapons offline, FTL disabled. The subsystem tracks communicate exactly how much capability the ship has left at any moment. This is the core design goal.

10. Weapons

Each ship carries a fixed loadout of weapons defined on its SSD. Weapons are divided into two categories: Primary and Secondary.

Primary Weapons

  • Every ship has exactly one Primary Weapon.
  • Primary weapons have no ammo limit and the repeatable trait.
  • A ship may fire its primary weapon multiple times per activation if it has sufficient attack resource power tokens.
  • The primary weapon is not degraded until the Weapons track's final threshold is crossed.
  • Primary weapons are typically mid-range and reliable—consistent damage dealers each round.

Secondary Weapons

  • A ship may carry zero or more Secondary Weapons.
  • Secondary weapons are limited by power cost and/or ammo.
  • They provide tactical tools: higher burst damage, extreme range, area effects, or situational advantages.
  • Secondary weapons degrade first as the Weapons track fills (see Section 9).

Torpedo Example

Torpedoes illustrate the design intent of secondary weapons:

  • High dice count — capable of generating many damage events.
  • Long range — engage at ranges primaries cannot reach.
  • Minimum range restriction — cannot fire at close-range targets; forces engagement distance management.
  • Limited ammo — creates a tactical "spend now or save it?" decision each round.

12. Scenarios

While simply destroying the enemy fleet is a valid victory condition, Nexus: Shattered Void is designed around objectives. The following standard scenarios can be played with any two factions.

Scenario 1: Total Annihilation

Setup: Standard deployment. Victory: The player who destroys the most total point value of enemy ships wins. Play until one fleet is completely destroyed, or until agreed time limit. Tie goes to the player with more HP remaining across their fleet.

Scenario 2: Nexus Fragment Capture

Setup: Place 3 Nexus Fragment objectives in the center of the board, spaced 12" apart. Victory: Control an objective by having a ship within 3" with no enemy ships within 3". Score 2 VP per round for each controlled objective. First to 12 VP wins, or highest at game end.

Scenario 3: Breakthrough

Setup: One player is the Attacker; the other is the Defender. Attacker deploys 8" from one edge; Defender deploys anywhere in the center 12" strip. Victory: Attacker scores 3 VP for each ship that exits through the Defender's table edge. Defender scores 1 VP per Attacker ship destroyed. Play 6 rounds.

Scenario 4: Escort Mission

Setup: Attacker has one Carrier or Dreadnought designated as the Escort Target. This ship begins 4" from Attacker's edge. Victory: Attacker scores 10 VP if the Escort Target exits through the opposite edge. Defender scores 10 VP if the Escort Target is destroyed. Play 8 rounds.
