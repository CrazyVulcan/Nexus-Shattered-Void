The Shattering

Ten thousand years ago, something activated within the galactic core. In the span of seven minutes, a weapon—or a reaction, or a god—tore apart eleven thousand inhabited star systems and scattered their matter across a 3,000 light-year radius. The event is known as the Shattering. Nobody knows who or what caused it. The Nexus Fragments are all that remain.

The Shattering — Full Entry

In the third age of recorded stellar history, before the current calendar of the Terran Republic, there existed what xenoarchaeologists call the "Chorus Era"—a period of approximately forty thousand years during which the galactic core was densely populated by unknown civilizations, collectively referred to in recovered fragments as the "First Speakers."

At the transition point between the Chorus Era and the current age, every instrument that has ever been pointed at that period of time registers the same thing: a total absence of data. Then, 9,847 years ago, the absence ends—and in its place is the Void.

What is known: in the space of approximately seven standard minutes (as reconstructed from synchronized pulsar timing), a region of space 3,200 light-years in diameter underwent catastrophic structural collapse. Stars were extinguished. Planets were vaporized. The fabric of hyperspace in the region became permanently unstable, creating what navigators now call "the Shatter Corridors"—treacherous routes that can halve transit times or destroy ships without warning.

"We did not survive the Shattering. We were outside it. The question that haunts every Republic historian: were we outside it by luck, or by design?"

— Academician Varos Selene, Third Republic Historical Commission

The Nexus Fragments are the only surviving artifacts from the pre-Shattering era. They range in size from dust particles to objects the size of small moons. They emit exotic radiation on no known spectrum. Proximity to them causes hallucinations, precognitive episodes, and—in extreme cases—spontaneous evolutionary mutation. Every faction wants them. Nobody agrees on why they exist.
